Why Did Pillar I and II Emerge?

Global tax rules were designed several decades ago for brick-and-mortar businesses. With the evolution of technology, companies started earning billions in a country without any physical presence.

Problem: Large MNEs shifting profits to low-tax jurisdictions, eroding national tax bases
Solution: OECD and G20 launched the BEPS project
Agreement: 2021 agreement on Two-Pillar solution
Participants: 130+ jurisdictions including all GCC countries

Pillar I: Origin and Purpose

Pillar I was developed under the OECD/G20 BEPS initiative as one part of a broader "Two-Pillar" solution aimed at tackling the tax challenges of the digital economy.

flag
Main Objective: Update rules around profit allocation and nexus so that large multinational enterprises are required to pay tax in countries where their customers or users are located
public
Digital Focus: Address concerns that highly digital companies were able to shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions despite generating substantial revenues in market countries
balance
Fair Sharing: Support a fairer sharing of taxing rights in today's digital economy

Pillar I: Key Features

Scope of Application

Pillar I targets only the largest and most profitable MNE groups:

1
Revenue Threshold: Annual global revenues over €20 billion
2
Profitability Test: Above 10% profitability (profit before tax ÷ revenue)
3
Country-Level Threshold: €1 million revenue (€250k for smaller economies)
4
Exclusions: Extractives (oil & gas) and regulated financial services

Amount A: Demystified

Core Concept

At the heart of Pillar I is "Amount A" - giving market countries (where customers/users are located) a new right to tax a share of the MNE's profit, even with no physical presence.

How Amount A Works

Step 1

Calculate Residual Profit

Determine profit above 10% margin
Example: If global margin is 17%, then 7% is residual profit

Step 2

Apply 25% Allocation

25% of residual profit is allocated to market jurisdictions

Step 3

Distribute to Countries

Each eligible country receives share based on revenue contribution

Qualification Thresholds

A country qualifies for Amount A if:

  • MNE has at least €1 million in revenue, OR
  • €250,000 for smaller countries (GDP under €40 billion)
  • No impact on revenues below €250,000 in any country

Marketing & Distribution Safe Harbour (MDSH)

Key Question

What if a market jurisdiction is already taxing some of those profits?

The Problem

  • Without adjustment, Amount A would add an extra layer of tax
  • Local distributor might already earn high profit margin under transfer pricing rules
  • Double taxation risk on the same residual profit

The Solution

  • MDSH caps or reduces Amount A allocation where market jurisdiction already taxes residual profits
  • Ensures local profits aren't taxed twice
  • Amount A only tops up what's missing

MDSH Application

Amount B: Simplified Returns, Fewer Disputes

Amount B is a Pillar I mechanism designed to simplify how countries tax routine sales, marketing & distribution functions by setting a fixed profit margin for routine activities.

flag
Key Difference: Amount B does not reallocate global profits. Instead, applies where local entity performs qualifying activities
description
Scope: Only when MNE has physical presence (subsidiary or PE) performing qualifying marketing/distribution activities
balance
Benefit: Standardized profit level regardless of internal benchmarking, giving certainty to tax authorities and taxpayers

Amount B Explained

check_circle

Applicability

In-scope transactions:

  • Buy-sell, commissionaire, or sales agent model for tangible goods
  • Distributor performs only routine functions (warehousing, invoicing, customer management)
  • Entity does not own valuable intangibles or assume complex risks
cancel

Exclusions

Excluded activities:

  • Retailers (if B2C sales >20%)
  • Digital services
  • Manufacturers

These involve higher complexity, valuable assets, or significant risks beyond routine distribution.

The Simplified Pricing Framework

Pricing Matrix

  • Return derived from a global dataset
  • Considers industry grouping & factor intensity
  • Basic goods distributor gets lower margin than high warehousing/logistics operations

Cap & Collar Mechanism

  • Cap: Maximum limit on profit margin
  • Collar: Minimum limit on profit margin
  • Keeps margins within reasonable band
  • Alternative caps for qualifying jurisdictions

Data Availability

  • Adjustments for jurisdictions lacking reliable data
  • Criteria to be defined by OECD
  • Special provisions for low-capacity jurisdictions

Amount A vs Amount B - Summary

looks_one

Amount A

  • Reallocates global profits to market jurisdictions
  • Applies without physical presence requirement
  • Based on customer/user location
  • 25% of residual profit above 10% margin
  • For largest MNEs (>€20B revenue)
looks_two

Amount B

  • Standardizes routine distribution profits
  • Requires physical presence (subsidiary/PE)
  • Based on local activities performed
  • Fixed profit margins from global matrix
  • Optional framework for countries

Impact on GCC Region

Regional Hub Implications

Many GCC jurisdictions act as regional hubs or sales locations for MNEs. Amount B may directly impact how these routine distribution functions are taxed, providing greater certainty and standardization.
