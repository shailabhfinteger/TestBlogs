Two-Pillar Tax Reform - Part II

A Beginner's Guide to Pillar II

International Tax • 15 min read

Pillar II: Origin and Purpose

Pillar II focuses on tackling the remaining issue of profit shifting to low-tax jurisdictions. Its objective is to introduce a 15% global minimum corporate tax rate, ensuring that large MNEs pay at least a minimum level of tax, no matter where they operate.

Development: Developed alongside Pillar I by OECD Inclusive Framework (2019-2020)
Origin: Started as anti-base erosion proposals
Minimum Rate: 15% global minimum tax to prevent race to the bottom
Agreement: 136 countries agreed in October 2021

Pillar II: Key Features

GloBE Rules Framework

Pillar II introduces a coordinated Global Anti-Base Erosion (GloBE) rules framework:

1
Scope: MNE groups with annual consolidated revenues > €750 million
2
Minimum ETR: 15% Effective Tax Rate calculated per jurisdiction
3
Top-Up Tax: Applied when ETR < 15% to reach minimum level
4
Per-Country Calculation: Cannot offset low-tax profits in one country with higher-tax profits in another

Scope and Exclusions

Excluded Entities

Understanding ETR (Effective Tax Rate)

What is ETR?

ETR under Pillar II is the core metric used to determine if a jurisdiction's profits are taxed at least at the 15% minimum rate.

ETR Calculation

ETR =
Covered Taxes
GloBE Income
Why Not Just Use Statutory Rate?
ETR reflects actual taxes paid relative to income, after adjusting for local incentives, deductions, and exemptions - providing a real-world view of tax levels.

Key Principle

Per-Jurisdiction Calculation
ETR is calculated separately for each jurisdiction where the MNE operates. If ETR is below 15%, a top-up tax applies. If it meets or exceeds 15%, no additional tax is required.

Covered Taxes - What's Included & Excluded

check_circle

Inclusions

  • Corporate income tax
  • Taxes on distributed profits (if aligned with rules)
  • Certain foreign taxes (e.g., withholding taxes if attributable to local income)
  • Deferred tax expenses (if measured at ≥15% minimum rate)
cancel

Exclusions

  • VAT / GST
  • Payroll taxes
  • Customs duties
  • Unrelated or non-business taxes

Decoding GloBE Income

GloBE income is the income base used to calculate the ETR under OECD Pillar II GloBE rules.

Key Principle

  • GloBE income DOES NOT equal taxable income under local tax law
  • Based on group's financial accounting (book) income
  • Specific adjustments defined by OECD Model Rules

Why Not Taxable Income?

  • Taxable income varies widely between countries due to local rules
  • Financial accounting income offers standardized, comparable base
  • Ensures uniformity and avoids local manipulation
  • Uses audited consolidated statements as foundation

Summary

GloBE income = standardized, adjusted financial accounting income, used as the base to calculate ETR under Pillar II. It is the "common ground" number that makes the top-up tax system work globally.

Substance-Based Carve-Outs

Core Concept

The substance-based income exclusion reduces the amount of profit subject to top-up tax based on the idea that MNEs should not pay top-up tax on routine profits from genuine economic activities.

Exclusions vs Carve-Outs

🚫

Exclusions

Entities or income fully outside the scope of GloBE from the start

Examples: governmental entities, non-profits, pension funds, international shipping income

✂️

Carve-Outs

Amounts in scope, subtracted from GloBE income before calculating ETR

Examples: fixed routine returns on payroll and tangible assets

Fixed Percentage Returns

Eligible Payroll Costs
10% routine return (decreasing to 5% over ten years)
Eligible Tangible Assets
8% routine return (decreasing to 5% over ten years)

Substance Carve-Out Example

Practical Scenario

Given

MNE in Country A

• GloBE income: €100 million
• Eligible payroll expenses: €30 million
• Eligible tangible assets: €50 million

Calculation

Carve-Out Computation

• Payroll carve-out: 10% × €30m = €3m
• Asset carve-out: 8% × €50m = €4m
• Total carve-out: €7m

Result

Adjusted Income for ETR Test

€100m - €7m = €93 million

This €93 million is tested against the 15% ETR. If ETR on €93m is below 15%, top-up tax applies.

Why Does It Matter?

Protection of Routine Business
Without the carve-out, even low-taxed income from real, on-the-ground business (factories, warehouses, employees) would face top-up tax. The carve-out ensures the 15% minimum tax applies only to excess profits, not routine economic returns.

Decoding QDMTT

home_work

Qualified Domestic Minimum Top-Up Tax

What it means: If an MNE group has a subsidiary in Country X with low ETR (<15%), Country X can apply its own QDMTT to collect the top-up tax locally.

Key benefit: Ensures profits earned locally are taxed up to 15% minimum before foreign parent country (via IIR) or other countries (via UTPR) can step in.

settings

How QDMTT Works

QDMTT follows same Pillar II principles as GloBE rules:

  • Applies 15% minimum rate
  • On the same GloBE income
  • With same covered taxes and adjustments
  • Must meet OECD QDMTT standard to be recognized

Decoding IIR - Income Inclusion Rule

Income Inclusion Rule is also known as the "Top-Down Rule".

What It Means

  • If MNE group has subsidiaries in jurisdictions where ETR < 15%
  • UPE jurisdiction applies top-up tax on those low-taxed profits
  • Ensures group's ETR in each jurisdiction meets 15% minimum standard
  • Source country liability doesn't change unless it has QDMTT

How It Works

  • IIR applies before UTPR (top-down approach)
  • Parent country collects top-up tax shortfall
  • Only if parent doesn't apply IIR do other countries use UTPR
  • Prevents profits from escaping 15% minimum

Decoding UTPR - Undertaxed Profits Rule

security

The "Backstop Rule"

What it means: If MNE group has low-taxed profits (ETR < 15%) and UPE jurisdiction doesn't apply IIR, then UTPR lets other countries step in.

Mechanism: Countries apply proportionate adjustments to collect remaining top-up tax needed to reach 15% minimum.

balance

Proportionate Adjustments

Methods:

  • Denying tax deductions (e.g., for intra-group payments)
  • Imposing equivalent taxes on local entities

Allocation based on:

  • Number of employees, OR
  • Value of tangible assets in jurisdiction

Decoding STTR - Subject to Tax Rule

STTR is a treaty-based rule designed to protect source countries (where income is earned), especially developing economies. It is separate from GloBE rules and operates alongside them.

flag
Purpose: Allows source country to apply withholding tax on cross-border intra-group payments if taxed below 9% in recipient's country
attach_money
Applies to: Interest, royalties, certain service fees between related entities
description
Requirement: Only applies when two countries have agreed to include it in their tax treaty
🔍
Independent: Not automatic and operates independently of €750M threshold

Broad Steps for Pillar II

Implementation Process

1
Threshold Check: Verify €750M MNE group consolidated revenue threshold
2
Entity Identification: Identify in-scope entities, apply exclusions
3
ETR Calculation: Calculate ETR per jurisdiction and compare to 15% minimum
4
Apply QDMTT: If top-up tax arises, apply QDMTT if available
5
Apply IIR/UTPR: If no QDMTT, apply IIR at UPE level; if no IIR, apply UTPR
6
Apply STTR: Parallel application on low-taxed cross-border payments under treaties

Transitional Measures and Safe Harbours

ETR Safe Harbours

  • De minimis: €1 million or less profit threshold
  • Simplified ETR test: ≥15% based on CbCR data
  • Routine profits only: Based on simplified tests
  • Avoids detailed ETR calculations in qualifying jurisdictions

Deferred Tax Relief

  • Accounts for deferred tax liabilities during transition
  • Includes eligible deferred tax from before Pillar II
  • Avoids unfair top-up taxes due to past timing differences
  • Measured at or above 15% minimum rate

Other Transitional Relief

  • Newly in-scope MNEs: Time to adjust to compliance requirements
  • Simplified carve-out rules: Easier calculation during initial years
  • Penalty relief: Reduced risk during transition period

GCC Implementation Status: Pillar II

Regional Update

Several GCC countries are in various stages of implementing Pillar II through domestic legislation. Implementation timelines vary by jurisdiction, with most targeting 2024-2025 effective dates for the core GloBE rules.
