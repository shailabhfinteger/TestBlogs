UAE Entity, India Exposure: Hyatt's Strategic Role Triggers Permanent Establishment, Says Supreme Court

A landmark Supreme Court decision on operational control and permanent establishment implications

IN A NUTSHELL

"The appellant did not merely have high-level strategic oversight. It exercised real-time control over hotel operations, including HR, procurement, and branding, through enforceable contractual rights."

"Remuneration linked to room revenue and operating profits demonstrates a commercial nexus beyond advisory services, reinforcing the inference of a PE."

"The existence and activities of a PE in India attract taxability independent of the global financial position of the enterprise. The attribution of profit flows from the business presence, not the group's profitability."

"A continuous and coordinated presence through rotating personnel can constitute a PE, even if no single individual satisfies the temporal threshold."

"The appellant had the right to implement Hyatt standards, appoint key personnel, and formulate operating policies. These were not passive advisory roles, but operationally significant functions."

KEY TAKEAWAYS

What triggered PE risk?

  • 20-year operating agreement - Long-term contractual control

  • Physical office not essential - Control and regularity of presence are key

  • Hands-on involvement in core hotel operations

  • Fees tied to hotel revenue & profit - Performance-based compensation

  • Staff presence via rotation or secondment

  • Indian JV/subsidiary? Not a shield from PE exposure

  • Global losses? Still taxable in India

What YOU should watch for

Long-term contracts that lock in operational authority (especially in branding, staffing, or day-to-day control) can signal that you're running the business, not just advising.

Regular access to client premises - Even without a lease or exclusive use of space, regular access to client premises where business is actually conducted may give rise to PE exposure.

Operational control indicators - If your team is helping hire the GM, set prices, manage procurement, approve SOPs, you're functionally running the show. Courts may not treat that as "just consulting".

Success-based fees mean skin in the game. When compensation is linked to business performance, it's harder to argue passive advisory.

Personnel rotation - It isn't just about one person's stay. Personnel rotating with a continuous on-ground presence handling core operations increases exposure.

Indian affiliate doesn't insulate the foreign parent if key decisions and operational control still originate from the same premises. Courts focus on substance over structure.

Losses at group level don't shield a PE. It's treated as a separately taxable presence, and attribution is based on activity (not consolidated results).

FACTS EXPLAINED

Taxpayer: Hyatt International Southwest Asia Ltd ("HISWA"), a company incorporated under DIFC Law No. 3 of 2006 in Dubai, United Arab Emirates ("UAE").

Tax residence: UAE under Article 4 of the Indiaâ€“UAE Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement ("DTAA").

Strategic Oversight Service Agreement ("SOSA")

HISWA entered into two SOSAs with Asian Hotels Ltd, India ("AHL"):

  • One for Hyatt Regency Delhi; and

  • The other for Hyatt Regency Mumbai.

Contract period: Initial term of 20 years, extendable by another 10 years by mutual agreement.

As per the SOSA, HISWA had the right to assign its own or affiliate employees to AHL [later reorganized, with Asian Hotels (North) Ltd continuing the hotel ownership], including those who would serve full-time in executive roles.

TERMS OF THE SOSA

Strategic planning

Implementing Hyatt brand standards & SOPs

Advising on HR, pricing, marketing, operations, procurement, & financial controls

Appointing the General Manager & other key hotel executives without consent of the hotel

Fee model: A percentage of the room revenue and operating profit (not a fixed consultancy fee)

HISWA was authorized to formulate policies governing the hotel's operating bank accounts

Contractually enforceable control over hotel operations

HISWA'S INDIA TAX POSITION

Filed tax return in India, claiming a refund of taxes withheld in India taking a position that income was not taxable in India due to:

  • Absence of a Permanent Establishment ("PE") in India.

  • DTAA override.

  • Services rendered from UAE with no fixed place of business in India.

WHY NO PE?

India-linked functions fail both prongs of the Article 5(1) PE test

  • No fixed place "at its disposal" in India;

  • No business was carried on "through" such a place.

Temporary visits, strategic guidance, and brand compliance oversight, especially when provided remotely, do not amount to a PE

No Service PE under Article 5(2)(i)

  • The cumulative stay of HISWA's employees in India never exceeded 9 months in any 12-month period.

  • SOSA permitted only temporary and discretionary visits; no obligation to station employees in India.

No involvement of HISWA in day-to-day operations

Day-to-day hotel ops were managed by a separate Indian company - Hyatt India Pvt Ltd, under a separate Hotel Operating Services Agreement ("HOSA")

HISWA relied on the Supreme Court decisions of E-Funds IT Solutions Inc (399ITR34) and Formula One World Championship Ltd (394 ITR 80) emphasizing that a PE requires (i) a specific, identifiable location; (ii) that location being "at the disposal" of the foreign enterprise and (iii) actual business being conducted through that location.

INDIA TAX AUTHORITY POSITION

HISWA's facts confirm the existence of a fixed place PE in India under Article 5(1) of the DTAA.

HISWA had:

  • A Business connection (a domestic law threshold that is wider than the treaty definition of PE) in India under the Indian tax law.

  • PE in India under Article 5(1) of the DTAA.

  • Income classifiable as royalty under Article 12 of the DTAA and fees for technical services under the Indian tax law.

HISWA exercised operational control through its role under the SOSA.

Hotel premises were effectively at the disposal of HISWA.

Senior personnel of HISWA had significant and regular presence at the hotel, and were engaged in functions central to hotel performance.

HISWA's fee being linked to operating performance indicated active participation, not passive advisory.

ITAT OBSERVATIONS AND RULING

The Income Tax Appellate Tribunal ("ITAT") is the second appellate authority for direct tax matters in India (below the State High Courts) and is the final fact-finding authority.

  • The ITAT relied on SC decision in Formula One and emphasized right of disposal and core business activity being conducted through the PE.

  • HISWA's control over strategic, financial, and operational decisions through SOSA surpassed mere advisory role.

  • Visits by multiple personnel, some staying up to 9 months, satisfied the functional presence test, even if no single employee crossed the threshold.

  • Rejected the artificial distinction between HISWA and Hyatt India Pvt Ltd for PE attribution purposes, noting interconnected functions.

Conclusion: HISWA had a Fixed Place PE in India. Business income attributable to that PE was taxable in India.

HIGH COURT OBSERVATIONS AND RULING

HISWA had contractually vested control over:

  • Appointment of General Manager & key personnel

  • HR, branding, pricing, marketing strategies and procurement

  • Operation of the hotel bank accounts

Fee model tied to hotel revenue & profits, suggesting direct commercial interest.

Distinguished E-Funds case: E-Funds involved auxiliary support services, unlike Hyatt's direct role in implementing strategy, managing operations, and enforcing brand & financial policies.

Rejected the idea that absence of designated physical space invalidated PE; relied on Formula One case to hold that shared, temporary or non-exclusive use is sufficient if business is conducted through it to establish PE.

The taxpayer argued that since it had incurred global losses, no income could be attributed to its alleged PE in India under Article 7. The High Court rejected this argument, holding that taxability depends on the existence and functions of the PE in India and not on the overall profitability of the foreign enterprise.

Conclusion: The taxpayer's activities transcended a mere advisory role and extended to direct operational control, satisfying the test for a Fixed Place PE.

THE SUPREME COURT JUDGMENT

Substantial question of law: Whether HISWA had a Fixed Place PE in India under Article 5(1) of the India-UAE DTAA?

OBSERVATIONS ON THE SOSA AGREEMENT

  • The SOSA was a long-term contract (20 years, extendable by 10) that granted HISWA substantive operational authority.

  • The agreement conferred HISWA with rights to:

    • Appoint and supervise key hotel personnel, including the General Manager;

    • Formulate and enforce policies on HR, procurement, branding, pricing, guest experience, and bank account operations;

    • Deploy personnel (including affiliate employees) at its discretion, without requiring consent from the hotel owner.

  • The consideration was linked to the hotel's financial performance (room revenue and gross operating profit) rather than a fixed fee. This underscored a commercially participative role.

OBSERVATIONS ON CONCEPT OF PE

The Court reaffirmed the principles laid down in Formula One case that a Fixed Place PE exists where a place is:

  • At the disposal of the foreign enterprise, and

  • Such place is used to carry on its business (wholly or partly).

The Supreme Court reiterated that the existence of a Fixed Place PE under Article 5(1) must be assessed using three key criteria:

Stability

A sustained and continuous arrangement for conducting business from a particular location

Productivity

Performance of revenue-generating functions at that location

Dependence

A business presence that is functionally reliant on the use of the premises

The fact that no single employee stayed in India beyond the 9 month threshold under Article 5(2)(i) was not decisive.

The Court observed that continuity of business operations (even via multiple personnel in rotation) was sufficient to support the existence of a PE.

ON THE MATTER OF ATTRIBUTION OF PROFITS DESPITE GLOBAL LOSSES

  • The Court rejected the argument that no attribution of profits could be made because the entity incurred global losses.

  • Citing the High Court's ruling (larger bench) on the same matter, the Supreme Court affirmed that taxability in India arises from the existence and activity of the PE, not from group-level profitability.

  • The PE is treated as a separately taxable presence in a foreign jurisdiction, and profits attributable to it can be taxed in India regardless of the foreign entity's overall financial position.

Conclusion: The Supreme Court held that HISWA had a Fixed Place PE in India under Article 5(1) of the India-UAE DTAA, based on its sustained operational control over key hotel functions through a long-term agreement. It clarified that legal ownership or exclusive possession of premises is not necessary - what matters is the right to access and use the premises in the course of business with a sufficient degree of permanence. The Court also rejected the plea that global losses preclude attribution, affirming that taxability flows from the existence and activities of the PE itself.
